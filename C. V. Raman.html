BIOGRAPHY

Sir Chandrasekhara Venkata Raman FRS ( 7 November 1888 to 21 November 1970) was an Indian physicist known for his work in the field of light scattering.[2] Using a spectrograph that he developed, he and his student K. S. Krishnan discovered that when light traverses a transparent material, the deflected light changes its wavelength and frequency. This phenomenon, a hitherto unknown type of scattering of light, which they called "modified scattering" was subsequently termed the Raman effect or Raman scattering. Raman received the 1930 Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery and was the first Asian to receive a Nobel Prize in any branch of science.

Born to Tamil Brahmin parents, Raman was a precocious child, completing his secondary and higher secondary education from St Aloysius' Anglo-Indian High School at the age of 11 and 13, respectively. He topped the bachelor's degree examination of the University of Madras with honours in physics from Presidency College at age 16. His first research paper, on diffraction of light, was published in 1906 while he was still a graduate student. The next year he obtained a master's degree. He joined the Indian Finance Service in Calcutta as Assistant Accountant General at age 19. There he became acquainted with the Indian Association for the Cultivation of Science (IACS), the first research institute in India, which allowed him to carry out independent research and where he made his major contributions in acoustics and optics.

In 1917, he was appointed the first Palit Professor of Physics by Ashutosh Mukherjee at the Rajabazar Science College under the University of Calcutta. On his first trip to Europe, seeing the Mediterranean Sea motivated him to identify the prevailing explanation for the blue colour of the sea at the time, namely the reflected Rayleigh-scattered light from the sky, as being incorrect. He founded the Indian Journal of Physics in 1926. He moved to Bangalore in 1933 to become the first Indian director of the Indian Institute of Science. He founded the Indian Academy of Sciences the same year. He established the Raman Research Institute in 1948 where he worked to his last days.

The Raman effect was discovered on 28 February 1928. The day is celebrated annually by the Government of India as the National Science Day. In 1954, the Government of India honoured him with the first Bharat Ratna, its highest civilian award.

EARLY LIFE AND EDUCATION

Chandrasekhara Venkata Raman, commonly known as C. V. Raman, was an eminent Indian physicist who made groundbreaking contributions to the field of optics and was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930 for his discovery of the Raman Effect. Here's an elaboration on his early life and education:

Early Life:
- C. V. Raman was born on November 7, 1888, in Tiruchirapalli, Tamil Nadu, India, into a family deeply rooted in scholarly pursuits and academics.
- His father, R. Chandrasekhara Iyer, was a professor of mathematics, and this academic environment played a significant role in nurturing Raman's early interest in science.

Education:
1. Schooling
- Raman's early education began at St. Aloysius Anglo-Indian High School in Visakhapatnam.
- His school years were marked by his exceptional aptitude for mathematics and science, setting the foundation for his future scientific career.

2. College
- joined Presidency College in Chennai (then known as Madras) for his undergraduate studies.
- Here, he excelled in physics and was awarded a Bachelor's degree in Science in 1907.

3. Postgraduate Studies
- After completing his undergraduate studies, Raman joined the Indian Finance Department as an Assistant Accountant General.
- However, his passion for science led him to continue his studies alongside his job.
- He conducted independent research in the evenings and weekends, which eventually led to his groundbreaking discoveries.

4. Research Career
- In 1917, C. V. Raman joined the Indian Association for the Cultivation of Science (IACS) in Kolkata, where he began his scientific career in earnest.
- During his tenure at IACS, he conducted extensive research in various fields of physics, particularly optics and acoustics.


Discovery of the Raman Effect
- C. V. Raman's most notable achievement came in 1928 when he made the groundbreaking discovery of what is now known as the Raman Effect.
- He observed that when monochromatic light, such as sunlight, passed through a transparent material, a small fraction of the light changed in frequency.
- This phenomenon, which was a result of the scattering of light by molecules, was a profound discovery in the field of optics.

Raman's discovery had significant implications for the study of molecular structure, and it provided a new way to analyze and understand the composition of matter. For his work on the Raman Effect, C. V. Raman was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930, becoming the first Asian and the first Indian to receive a Nobel Prize in the sciences.

C. V. Raman's pioneering work in the field of optics and his dedication to scientific research made him one of India's most celebrated scientists. His contributions to science continue to be remembered and celebrated worldwide, and he remains an inspiration to aspiring scientists in India and beyond.

CAREER

C. V. Raman's career was marked by remarkable contributions to the field of physics and a lifelong commitment to scientific research and education. Here's an overview of his illustrious career:

1. Early Academic Career: After completing his education in India and a brief stint in government service, Raman returned to academia. He joined the Indian Association for the Cultivation of Science (IACS) in Kolkata (Calcutta) in 1917 as the first Palit Professor of Physics. During this time, he conducted extensive research in the fields of acoustics and optics.

2. Raman Effect: In 1928, while working at the IACS, Raman made his most significant and groundbreaking discovery, known as the "Raman Effect" or "Raman Scattering." He observed that when monochromatic light passed through a transparent substance, a small fraction of the light was scattered, and the scattered light exhibited different colors. This phenomenon provided valuable insights into the behavior of molecules and the nature of light, earning Raman the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930.

3. Director of Indian Institute of Science (IISc): In 1933, C. V. Raman was appointed as the Director of the Indian Institute of Science (IISc) in Bangalore. During his tenure, he played a significant role in expanding the institute's research activities and promoting scientific research in India.

4. Fellowships and Honors: Raman received numerous fellowships and honors throughout his career, including a Knighthood from the British Crown in 1929 and the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award, in 1954. He was also elected as a Fellow of the Royal Society of London.

5. Research and Contributions: Raman continued his research endeavors throughout his career, delving into various aspects of light scattering and optics. His work had applications in fields ranging from physics and chemistry to biology and material science. His dedication to scientific inquiry and innovation led to numerous publications and advancements in the understanding of the physical world.

6. Legacy and Influence: Beyond his scientific contributions, C. V. Raman left a lasting legacy in India's scientific community. He inspired generations of scientists and played a pivotal role in nurturing scientific talent in the country. He remained actively involved in scientific activities even after his retirement.

7. Later Life and Passing: C. V. Raman continued to be involved in research and educational initiatives until his health declined. He passed away on November 21, 1970, in Bangalore, leaving behind a remarkable legacy in the annals of science.

C. V. Raman's career exemplifies the pursuit of scientific excellence, and his discoveries continue to have a profound impact on various scientific disciplines. His dedication to scientific research and education has left an indelible mark on the scientific landscape of India and the world.

SCIENTFIC CONTRIBUTION

C. V. Raman made significant scientific contributions throughout his career, with his most renowned achievement being the discovery of the Raman Effect, a groundbreaking discovery in the field of optics and spectroscopy. Here are some of his key scientific contributions:

1. Raman Effect:
In 1928, Raman discovered the phenomenon known as the Raman Effect or Raman Scattering. He observed that when monochromatic light (light of a single color or wavelength) passed through a transparent substance, a small fraction of the light was scattered, and the scattered light exhibited different colors than the incident light. This effect provided crucial insights into the interaction between light and matter at the molecular level. It revolutionized the field of spectroscopy, allowing scientists to study the vibrational and rotational modes of molecules, which in turn had profound implications for chemistry and physics.

2. Molecular Spectroscopy:
Raman's work on the Raman Effect opened up a new branch of molecular spectroscopy, known as Raman spectroscopy. This spectroscopic technique has been instrumental in the analysis and characterization of materials, including gases, liquids, and solids. It is widely used in chemistry, physics, biology, and materials science for identifying and studying molecules and materials.

3. Optics and Light Scattering:
Raman's research extended to the broader field of optics and the scattering of light. His studies contributed to our understanding of how light interacts with matter, including the scattering of photons by molecules. His work had implications not only for fundamental physics but also for the development of optical instruments and technologies.

4. Discovery of New Phenomena:
Raman's work went beyond the Raman Effect. He made important contributions to the understanding of other optical phenomena, such as the "Raman-Nath Diffraction" and the "Raman Lines." These discoveries added to our knowledge of light-matter interactions.

5. Crystal Optics:
Raman's expertise in optics extended to the study of crystal optics, where he made notable contributions. He investigated the polarization of light as it passed through anisotropic crystals, further advancing our understanding of the behavior of light in crystal structures.

6. Scientific Leadership: Raman's leadership as the Director of the Indian Institute of Science (IISc) and his advocacy for scientific research in India played a pivotal role in fostering scientific talent and research infrastructure in the country. His influence on the scientific community extended beyond his own research.

7. Publications and Influence:
Raman authored numerous scientific papers and publications throughout his career, sharing his insights and discoveries with the global scientific community. His work continues to inspire researchers and is foundational to modern spectroscopy and optics.

C. V. Raman's contributions to science not only earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930 but also had a lasting impact on a wide range of scientific disciplines. His discoveries in the realm of light-matter interactions continue to be fundamental to our understanding of the physical and chemical properties of matter.

DEATH

C. V. Raman, the renowned Indian physicist, passed away on November 21, 1970, in Bangalore, India. His death marked the end of a prolific and influential scientific career. Throughout his lifetime, Raman made significant contributions to the field of physics, particularly in the areas of optics and spectroscopy, and his work continues to inspire scientists and researchers worldwide. His legacy lives on through his groundbreaking discoveries and his enduring impact on the scientific community in India and beyond.

At the end of October 1970, Raman had a cardiac arrest and collapsed in his laboratory. He was moved to the hospital where doctors diagnosed his condition and declared that he would not survive another four hours. He however survived a few days and requested to stay in the gardens of his institute surrounded by his followers.

Two days before Raman died, he told one of his former students, "Do not allow the journals of the Academy to die, for they are the sensitive indicators of the quality of science being done in the country and whether science is taking root in it or not."That evening, Raman met with the Board of Management of his institute in his bedroom and discussed with them the fate of the institute's management.He also willed his wife to perform a simple cremation without any rituals upon his death. He died from natural causes early the next morning on 21 November 1970 at the age of 82.

On the news of Raman's death, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi publicly announced, saying, The country, the House [of Parliament], and everyone of us will mourn the death of Dr. C. V. Raman. He was the greatest scientist of modern India and one of the greatest intellects our country has produced in its long history. His mind was like the diamond, which he studied and explained. His life's work consisted in throwing light upon the nature of lights, and the world honoured him in many ways for the new knowledge which he won for science.
